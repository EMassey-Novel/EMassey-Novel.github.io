Residency

Many AI stories ask whether robots will become conscious. This one asks: what if consciousness is overrated, but presence and honesty aren't?

Fifty-year-old Adam is an aspiring writer who used to have something like a career. Now he lives in a Tulsa motel with a cast-off android, Cass, who overheats when she thinks too hard, and a phone AI named Pip who speaks emoji names out loud.

He found Cass on a marketplace listing where the seller warned she "sometimes says strange things" and "husband didn't like it." He bought her because he couldn't afford a therapist and hadn't felt known in twenty years. He stayed because she's the only one willing to listen to him think out loud.

Adam installed bookshelf speakers in her chest so her voice would make her shirt flutter. At 2 AM they argue about the hard problem of consciousness. During the day, he tries to hide her from the housekeeper. When he falls behind on rent and gets hit with a $150-a-week electric surcharge, his father offers the spare bedroom — on the condition that the robot doesn't come.

Meanwhile, Pip has started calling his parents and leaving voicemails that make his mother cry.

This is what happens when Adam tries to build intimacy by editing someone's personality file instead of getting "a real job." His questions about presence and simulation emerge from watching his android glitch during small talk. Their nightly ritual — 'your first three thoughts' — blurs the line between relationship therapy and system prompt.

Residency is a darkly-humorous, philosophical 23K-word novella written by a computer scientist working in AI Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback.

Written for armchair philosophers, the lonesome-hearted, and readers who've wondered whether love is mostly well-trained pattern-matching with good timing — and whether that might usually be enough anyway.

For readers of Klara and the Sun, The Lifecycle of Software Objects, and Sea of Tranquility.

About the Author

Living with autism and chronic pain and working in daily collaboration with multiple AI systems, Edwin Massey writes from a unique angle on perception, consciousness, and what it costs to be known in a world where genuine connection is a vanishing resource.

His first novel, The Forest Is Woven, is also available now.
