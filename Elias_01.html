01: Elias, 2063

The forest is woven. Roots thread in a quiet pattern, branching like nerves in an unseen network.

The scanned image sat on his screen in small, angled handwriting. A single, complete sentence that felt less written in the past than waiting for him now.

Elias felt a familiar pull. Sharper than it had been in weeks. Not curiosity. A physical ache, the kind that starts in the chest and settles behind the sternum.

He waited for his internal voice to chime in: you're doing it again, building meaning out of noise. But it stayed quiet. This unnerved him more than any scolding.

Something in him answered the words on the screen. Years ago he'd read a battered collection of Aristotle, and descriptions of aether as the unseen, incorruptible element of the heavens—a medium in which light and stars and mysteries moved.

Then the moment closed, but he continued reading.

He sat too long, shoulders curled over the terminal, until his spine sent up its usual flares. Neck and shoulders first. Then the knotted band at the base of his skull. The low back that never forgave the last surgery.

He pushed himself upright with care. Stretched his neck until vertebrae clicked in a familiar sequence. He looked up at the peeling ceiling and held the position. Hairline fractures spidering through old plaster, catching faint light, making thin shadows that looked like an aerial view of dried riverbeds.

In the loft around him, vegetation racks hummed along the length of one wall, and shelves of old books lined another. Soft morning light came through the tall east window where he'd hauled the thick curtains open. He'd paid extra for the top floor. It gave his thoughts more vertical room, a sense that when they rose they weren't crushed by someone else's footsteps.

He crossed the room, socked feet shuffling on the smooth boards. On most days he didn't have to see anyone, and he wore the same type of clothes: a long‑sleeved shirt in a dark blue, soft enough that he didn't feel the seams, and loose linen pants that breathed without scratching. It had taken years of trial and error to find fabrics that didn't make him irritable. No slimy synthetics, no coarse wool turning every movement into sandpaper.

He knew every floorboard that creaked, and the way sounds bounced off every corner of the room. No overhead fluorescents, and no cold white LEDs. Golden hour sun when he could get it, and a handful of hoarded amber bulbs for every other hour.

He'd finally crafted a space around himself—the way one could only after he stopped arranging himself around someone else's space.

It had taken decades for him to learn that his exhaustion was not a failure of character, but the cost of operating his nervous system in a world built for someone else. He’d spent his early life trying to fit inside its noise and its constant demands, until he simply couldn’t.

The only companion who had never asked him to translate himself was a dog named Silas. He had died four years ago, a quiet cessation of breathing on the rug near the couch. Elias still stepped carefully around that patch of floor, honoring a specific absence that no one else could measure.

The light slid across the custom wall‑mounted hydroponic rack, turning water droplets into tiny lenses. The micro cultivars were planted from knee-high up to where he could reach with a small stepladder. Baby carrots, dwarf bush beans, peppers, and bitter greens. Seven tobacco varieties, plus Nicotiana rustica. Medicinal herbs he trusted more than anything packaged. All of it threaded with translucent nutrient lines that pulsed in slow, regular beats, steadying something in him he didn't have a name for.

The smell—damp soil, chlorophyll, faint spice—cut through the old wood and dust of the building. On the windowsill, a small kalanchoe in a chipped mug held a tight cluster of florets—orange, darkening to red at the edges. Below them, the scalloped leaves repeated their own curve at every scale. He thumbed a leaf just to feel something that didn't care what anyone thought about it.

Below the rack, the workbench held his other obsessions in a loose heap. An old tape deck with coils of magnetic tape in labeled cases. A stack of dog‑eared printouts where ambient pump noise and street drones had been turned into visual waveforms. A wall‑size paper map of the neighborhood marked with tiny crosses where, according to a hobbyist outage feed, street cameras and public overlays had gone dark, been hacked, or otherwise glitched. Up here, in the part of the hillside the maintenance droids overlooked, things still grew without permission.

"What time is it?" he asked, more to confirm that Echo was functional than to check the minute of the hour.

"7:07 a.m.," Echo replied through the custom‑built speakers scattered around the loft. It sounded like she was glad to be of some use.

"Another night survived," he muttered, the words dry in a throat that had only known a few hours of sleep.

He'd been in a fog for weeks. Not the acute kind—he knew that one intimately—but the low, chronic variety. Thinking was possible, but costly. Every task drew against tomorrow's capacity.

His thoughts were a hand‑cranked flashlight on a moonless night over a shallow swamp. He could see just enough to stumble forward, but every crank of the handle required exhausting effort. Stopping meant complete darkness, with God‑knows‑what in the murky water at his feet.

When all he could manage was to lie on the couch, he read the ancient mystics. The ones who talked about seeing through this world into the one underneath it. Their certainty that patterns lay above apparent meaninglessness mirrored something in him he couldn't name. On his worst nights he suspected the mystics were wrong about everything. On his very worst nights he knew they were right, but that didn't solve anything.

Old neurology called it 'neuroplasticity.' The wellness system logged it as 'persistent recalibration risk.' He called it staying awake.

His self‑inventory reported baseline pain. He and baseline pain had known each other long enough to become uncomfortable partners. One was always insistent and the other was always tired.

This was the gift of the last five years—the pain had stopped surprising him, the way all the other things had stopped surprising him eventually, after he had tried hard enough and long enough to be the kind of person they didn't hurt.

He crossed to the counter, dialed a low dose on the old metal extractor, and watched the needles on its analog gauges climb as it pulled nicotine out of a tobacco leaf he'd hung to dry on his balcony. He put a few drops under his tongue. It hit his bloodstream with the usual bitter tingle. It should have smoothed him out. Instead his thoughts sped, circling back to the text waiting on his paper‑thin display.

He made himself eat. A bowl of peppers, greens, and cherry tomatoes. Too much salt. Not enough garlic. A meal that existed on principle rather than merit.

The trade‑deal tomatoes had arrived in their usual reusable bag outside his door. He wasn't a social person, but he couldn't turn down a touchless, regular delivery of good veg.

Somewhere beyond his wall, a neighbor's news stream echoed through the plumbing.

"…troubled systems consultant… long‑time critic of augmentation… found dead in his home…"

"…apparent suicide after spreading dangerous misinformation about aug tech…"

He knew that name. Three years ago they'd traded messages about algorithmic bias in augmentation screening. Then silence. Now this, delivered in the kind of voice focus groups had agreed made people more likely to accept bad news.

"That's not news," he said aloud. "That's narrative control."

Echo's response lagged. Just a fraction. Just enough that he noticed before he could decide not to.

"Do you want the microphone muted?" she asked.

He filed the lag without examining it. The gap between stimulus and response was where the architecture hid its adjustments. "No," he said.

He dried his hands on the least‑damp towel and went back to his long terminal desk. His two displays waited, their color balance nudged warmer than factory default. Status indicators blinked in three colors on her casing: green for processing, amber for output, blue for idle. The idle screen cooled briefly to a black mirror, catching only the dim amber light before waking to his input. On one side, the scanned pages sat open. On the second, a small analysis window compared the content to a stack of old texts he'd been feeding into the system for years.

The message had come in earlier that morning as encrypted text using a protocol deprecated in 2051. The terminal chimed, and at that exact instant the lowest pump on the wall skipped a cycle. He knew why. The building's mesh still routed legacy priority over the local timing bus. When the old handshake hit, it stole the sync pulse. The pump didn't lose power. It lost its beat for one cycle. The architecture claimed the old layers were erased, but the mesh still stuttered around them.

The industry split the stack cleanly: the mesh was the routing layer—sensors, fiber trunks, compliance gates carrying raw signal. The architecture was the predictive spine on top, deciding what to amplify, what to smooth, what to quietly erase. He'd learned the hard way that touching one always tripped the other.

The notification icon for that layer belonged to the kind of work he'd sworn off years ago. Anonymous clients on underground boards. People who traded in deleted logs and impossible data restoration all knew his username. He was the one who could pull records out of corporate archives and the architecture's own memory. He'd walked away after he realized he had spent all his time helping other people find what they were looking for.

The header timestamp read 2063‑03‑12 05:15.

New client. Will pay triple your rate. Decode the attached.

Meet me. No AI.

No name. No explanation.

Nobody with a normal problem would have sent it. It was the kind of thing someone did after they'd spent sleepless nights trawling old threads about a nameless analyst who could read between erased lines.

His eyes lifted automatically to the speaker casing on the desk, watching the dormant amber ring before he forced his gaze back down. He'd read that last line twice, feeling his shoulders tighten. People who didn't want Echo listening usually fell into two categories: paranoids who thought any local system was a backdoor to the grid, and architects who knew exactly how true that could be.

Whoever had sent this knew which category they belonged to. And they'd known exactly where to go looking for the ghost who used to answer to that icon.

At a glance, the content looked like a student's nature story. Simple sentences about forest floors and light. His attention went to the second paragraph. Tighter script, still legible:

At the base of a fallen tree nearby, he saw mushrooms—small, pale, growing in a curved line. He knelt and dug carefully with his fingers. Beneath them he found white threads, each one thinner than a hair, running from the roots of the fallen tree to the roots of three others that were still standing. He traced them with one finger. The dead tree's roots were wrapped in them. The living trees' roots were wrapped in them. They were connected.

He read on. The writer's name sat in half‑faded ink at the top of the first page: M. Cross.

He could feel his brain already starting to map sentence lengths, repeated words, where she broke the line, shoving them against old routing diagrams and half‑abandoned projects whether he liked it or not. This text wasn't just words. It felt like a pattern waiting to be seen from the right angle. Like circuitry only visible on the back of a board. He knew that feeling had betrayed him before. It didn't stop it from lighting up.

"Did you read it?" he asked.

"I'm aware of everything in your feed," Echo said. "It does not match known templates."

He exhaled at that. "No one would ask for this to be analyzed unless they had reason to believe there is something in it."

He reached for his nicotine again out of habit. He thought of a line from a mystic who wrote like a topologist. He'd read him at four in the morning once: the invisible tapestry behind the world. Theologically sloppy. Technically useless. But the image had stuck because it matched something he'd been chasing for years without language. Now Mara's first lines—forest floor, woven, roots and threads—sat down next to that image with a small, electrical click. It felt less like discovery than like remembering.

"I don't know what it is," he said. "But I'll take the work. Ask for a meeting in a public place. The diner on the hill near the station, I guess. If nothing else I'll get a real coffee out of it."

His words went out. The text stayed alive behind his eyes like an afterimage. For the first time in a long time, the darkness in his head wasn't just static. There was a feeling he could, maybe, walk toward. Not just a puzzle. A chance that the world might feel a little less thin.

He saved the file, set a reminder to check for a reply in the afternoon, and forced himself through the motions of the morning: hydroponic checks, a shower his joints complained about, clothes that didn't scratch. Trying not to rehearse a conversation he might never have. The bathroom mirror showed him what years of bad sleep looked like on a man past fifty. Close‑cropped hair receding on top, mostly grey at the temples. The uncomfortable appearance of someone who ate only because he had to. He looked away before he could inventory anything else.

When he passed the workbench, his gaze caught the edge of an old printout where street‑noise waveforms marched across the page, abandoned mid‑experiment.

He could imagine himself already reaching for that internal flashlight again. Already cranking it. Knowing from experience it would burn out the minute he stopped to rest. But for now, the beam reached a few feet forward. Enough to show a path from his loft to a diner he didn't like, a client he didn't know, and a signal he could feel but not name.
